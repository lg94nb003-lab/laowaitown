    24-Hour Transit Visa-Free Policy 2026: Universal Short-Layover Guide

    7 min read By LAOWAITOWN Editorial

    Quick Answer

    Holders of nearly every passport can transit through mainland China visa-free for up to 24 hours on a continuous onward journey to a third country or region. No advance application is needed; the permit is granted on arrival.

    • ✓ Open to nationals of almost any country (very few exceptions)
    • ✓ Maximum 24 hours, counted from arrival to departure
    • ✓ Confirmed onward ticket to a third country / region required

    Who is eligible?

    The 24-hour transit policy is the most permissive of China's transit rules. Unlike the 240-hour version, it is not limited to a specific country list — practically any passport holder with a valid onward ticket qualifies.

    You qualify if:

    1. You hold a valid passport (any nationality recognised by China).
    2. You will depart within 24 hours of arrival, on a confirmed onward flight, train or ship.
    3. Your onward destination is a third country or region different from your country of departure (Hong Kong, Macau and Taiwan count as separate regions).

    Key restrictions and exit-zone rules

    The 24-hour policy is generous on eligibility but stricter on movement. The rules depend on the port:

    • Most ports — stay airside. At many smaller airports, 24-hour transit passengers must remain in the international transit area and cannot enter the city.
    • Major hubs — limited city access. A growing list of major hubs (Beijing PEK/PKX, Shanghai PVG/SHA, Guangzhou CAN, Shenzhen SZX, Chengdu CTU/TFU, Xi'an XIY and others) issue a temporary entry permit that lets you leave the airport within the city limits.
    • No province-hopping. Unlike 240-hour transit, you cannot travel to another province during the 24-hour window. You must exit from the same port at which you arrived.

    If you anticipate needing to leave the airport or travel further, the 240-hour transit policy is almost always the better option.

    How it works at the airport

    1. Before boarding: tell your airline at check-in that you are using 24-hour transit. The airline will verify your onward ticket and route.
    2. On arrival in China: follow signs to Transit or Foreigner channels. At most airports there is a dedicated 24-hour transit desk near immigration.
    3. Show your documents: passport, confirmed onward ticket (paper or e-ticket) and, if applicable, the airside transit boarding pass.
    4. Receive a temporary entry permit or transit stamp: at hubs that allow city access, a permit is issued. At airside-only ports, no stamp is needed — you simply wait in the international transit lounge.
    5. Departure: board your onward flight within 24 hours of arrival. Missing this window can result in fines and an exit-entry record.

    Required documents

    • Valid passport (at least 6 months remaining recommended)
    • Confirmed onward ticket to a third country or region (paper or e-ticket)
    • Boarding pass for the onward flight, if already issued

    Common mistakes to avoid

    • Returning to the country of departure. An itinerary like Tokyo → Beijing → Tokyo does not qualify as transit. The onward destination must be different.
    • Assuming you can leave the airport everywhere. Airside-only ports do not allow city access. Check your specific airport in advance.
    • Confusing with 240-hour transit. If you want to leave the airport, travel between cities or stay longer than a day, apply for 240-hour transit at the same desk — it is free, requires the same documents and is available to 55 nationalities.
    • Missing the 24-hour deadline. The clock starts at arrival, not at the end of the day. A 22:00 arrival means you must depart by 22:00 the next day.
    • Booking separate tickets without a confirmed connection. Two unrelated tickets may not be accepted as "transit". Buy a through-ticket or confirm with the airline.

    Frequently asked questions

    Can I leave the airport during a 24-hour transit?

    It depends on the port. Major hubs such as Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, Shenzhen, Chengdu, Xi'an and others issue a temporary entry permit that allows movement within the city. Smaller airports require you to stay airside. Always confirm with the airline before booking.

    Does my country need to be on a specific list?

    No. The 24-hour policy is universal — almost every passport is accepted. The very few exceptions are countries without recognised travel documents accepted by Chinese immigration; these are extremely rare.

    What if my onward flight is cancelled and I overstay?

    Notify the local Public Security Bureau Exit-Entry Administration before the deadline expires. With proof of force majeure (cancelled flights, airline delays, illness), they typically grant a short extension free of charge. Overstaying without notification can lead to fines and a future entry record.

    Does the 24-hour clock start at landing or at midnight?

    It starts at the moment of arrival — not at midnight. If you arrive at 14:30, your onward flight must depart by 14:30 the next day at the latest.

    Can I switch from 24-hour to 240-hour transit on arrival?

    Yes. If your nationality is among the 55 countries eligible for the 240-hour transit policy and you meet the conditions (onward ticket within 240 hours, designated port), simply request 240-hour transit at the desk — there is no fee to switch.

    Is Hong Kong, Macau or Taiwan considered a "third region"?

    Yes. Hong Kong SAR, Macau SAR and Taiwan are all treated as separate regions for transit purposes. An itinerary like Bangkok → Shanghai → Hong Kong qualifies for either 24-hour or 240-hour transit.
