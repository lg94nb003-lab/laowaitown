    China Visa Types 2026: Complete Comparison

    10 min read By LAOWAITOWN Editorial

    Quick Answer

    China issues 17 ordinary visa categories, identified by letters and numbers (e.g. L for tourism, Z for work, X1 for long-term study). The latest addition is the K visa for young foreign science and technology talent, which took effect on October 1, 2025.

    • ✓ Letter = purpose (L = tourism, M = business, Z = work...)
    • ✓ Number 1 = long-term (> 180 days), 2 = short-term (≤ 180 days)
    • ✓ Long-term visas (X1, Z, J1, S1, Q1) require a follow-up Residence Permit within 30 days of arrival

    How the visa code system works

    China's ordinary visa system uses two layers. The letter identifies why you are coming (tourism, work, study, family, transit). The number distinguishes long-term from short-term cases:

    • Letter only (L, M, F, Z, C, G, D, R, K) — single category, no long/short split.
    • Letter + 1 (X1, S1, Q1, J1) — long-term, more than 180 days, normally followed by a Residence Permit.
    • Letter + 2 (X2, S2, Q2, J2) — short-term, up to 180 days, no Residence Permit needed.

    Diplomatic, courtesy and service visas are separate from the ordinary system and are not covered here.

    K visa — the newest addition (Oct 2025)

    The K visa was approved by the State Council in August 2025 and took effect on October 1, 2025. It is China's response to global competition for young science, technology, engineering and mathematics (STEM) talent.

    • Target applicants: young foreign nationals working or studying in STEM fields, including recent graduates of recognized universities and early-career researchers.
    • What's different: compared to the existing R visa (high-level talent) and Z visa (employer-sponsored), K does not require an existing Chinese employer or pre-issued Work Permit. Eligibility is talent-based.
    • Stay flexibility: multi-entry validity with longer single-stay periods than ordinary M or F visas.
    • Activities allowed: attending sci-tech conferences, joint research, technical exchanges, and limited paid sci-tech work.

    Detailed application criteria are administered by the National Immigration Administration in coordination with the Ministry of Science and Technology. Always verify the latest implementation rules through the NIA before applying — provincial pilots may follow different timelines.

    How to choose: 4-question decision tree

    1. Will you be paid by a Chinese entity? — Yes → Z (or K for sci-tech). No → continue.
    2. Will you stay longer than 180 days? — Yes → likely X1, S1, Q1, J1, R or D (long-term family/study/work series). No → continue.
    3. Why are you visiting? — Tourism → L · Business → M · Study → X2 · Family → S2 / Q2 · Journalism → J2 · Exchange/Research (unpaid) → F.
    4. Are you holding a Chinese Permanent Residence (Green Card) approval? — Yes → D, then apply for the PR card itself.

    Common matchups

    • Conference attendee, paid abroad: M visa.
    • Visiting your Chinese spouse: Q1 (long-term) or Q2 (short visit).
    • Visiting a foreign friend studying in China: S2 (short) or L (tourism, simpler).
    • Hired by Chinese tech firm, full-time: Z, then Residence Permit.
    • Postdoc invited by a Chinese university (STEM): R or K, depending on the program.

    Common mistakes to avoid

    • Using L for business meetings. Tourist visas do not cover commercial meetings or contract signing. M is the right code, even for short trips.
    • Mixing up X1 / X2. Programs longer than 180 days require X1 plus a Residence Permit. Short language courses fit X2.
    • Forgetting the 30-day Residence Permit window. Z, X1, J1, S1, Q1, R, K holders must apply for a Residence Permit within 30 days of arrival, not at the end of the visa.
    • Confusing K with R. R is for highest-level talent at recognized institutions; K is broader and aimed at younger researchers.
    • Treating D as a working visa. D is for those who have already been approved for permanent residence — it is the gateway, not the end goal.

    Translation: "I am applying for a Chinese [visa type] visa." Replace [签证类型] with the letter code, e.g. M, Z, K.

    Frequently asked questions

    If my country has a 30-day visa-free policy with China, do I still need a visa?

    For trips up to 30 days for tourism, business, family or transit, no — the unilateral visa-free policy covers you. For longer stays, work, paid study or journalism, you still need the corresponding visa (Z, X1, J, etc.).

    Can I switch from an L visa to a Z visa inside China?

    In most cases no. Z visas must be obtained outside China after your employer secures a Notice of Work Permit. There are very limited exceptions for specific talent programs.

    Is the K visa already accepting applications?

    The K visa is officially in force since October 1, 2025. Implementation is rolling out at major Chinese embassies and consulates. Always check the current acceptance status with your local consular service before lodging an application.

    Can a single visa allow multiple entries?

    Yes. Many M, F and the new K visas are issued multi-entry. Long-term codes (Z, X1, J1, S1, Q1) typically allow one entry on the visa itself, after which the Residence Permit governs entries.

    What happens if I overstay because I picked the wrong visa?

    You will face overstay penalties regardless of the reason. Contact the Public Security Bureau Exit-Entry Administration before your visa expires to request an extension or correction. See our extension & overstay guide.
